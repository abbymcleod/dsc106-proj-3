Writeup

During the data exploration phase of the project, we examined temperature trends across different cities and noticed a clear difference in projected warming patterns between cities on the East Coast and West Coast. Once we identified this trend, we wanted to design a visualization that would clearly communicate increases in temperature over time while still allowing users to closely inspect the data for individual cities.

One alternative visualization we considered focused on wet-bulb temperature, which combines surface temperature and humidity i nto a measure of heat stress. Our idea was to analyze whether certain cities would cross dangerous heat thresholds under different climate conditions. However, after experimenting with this approach, we decided not to use it because the visualization was relatively simple and functioned more like a static graph with minor interactive features added to it. We wanted a design where the interaction itself played a major role in how users explored and understood the data.

As a result, we chose our current visualization because it better supported meaningful interactivity and exploration. Our visual encoding was designed so that each city is represented by a specific color based on its projected winter warming. This allows users to quickly identify regional patterns and compare levels of warming across locations. When a user clicks on a city, the interface generates a detailed plot showing winter mean temperature by year, making it easy to examine long-term temperature trends for that specific location.

For the interaction techniques, we included buttons at the top of the visualization that allow users to switch between different emissions scenarios. This design choice lets users compare how projected warming changes depending on the climate scenario being viewed. The combination of clickable cities and selectable emissions scenarios creates an experience that would not be possible in a purely static graph, since users can dynamically explore multiple cities and compare different future outcomes in real time.

For this project, each team member developed their own interactive visualization, and as a group we evaluated which plots best satisfied the project requirements while also presenting the data in the clearest and most concise way. After comparing our ideas, we selected the visualizations that were the most interactive, informative, and visually effective for communicating our findings.

In total, the development process took approximately 3.5–4 people-hours. The most time-consuming aspect of the project was data wrangling and data exploration. Because the dataset was very large, simply loading, cleaning, and organizing the data required a significant amount of effort before visualization development could begin. Another major portion of the development time was spent refining the visualizations themselves, including making small adjustments to layout, formatting, and interactivity so the plots would present the findings in a clean, organized, and visually appealing manner.
